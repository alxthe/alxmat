foundations of flourishing elderflower, a symbol of life

on the two pillars of sustainable wellbeing

What does it mean to truly flourish? Not just to succeed, not just to be happy, but to experience the deep, sustainable wellbeing that comes from living in alignment with our deepest values and highest potential? After years of exploring this question through philosophy, psychology, and lived experience, I've come to believe that genuine flourishing rests on two fundamental pillars: self-security and creating a path towards transcendence.

Self-Security

Self-security is fundamentally being okay as you are. This comes from meeting core psychological needs, things like safety, belonging, autonomy, and competence (but also physiological ones like sleep, shelter, food, etc.)1. True self-security creates the foundation necessary for genuine growth and transformation. Carl Rogers captured this paradox perfectly:

"The curious paradox is that when I accept myself just as I am, then I can change."

Self-security creates inner wholeness. This is the capacity to hold all aspects of ourselves (strengths, weakness, etc.) in compassionate awareness. The psychologist Carl Jung wrote extensively about this process of individuation–becoming whole by integrating the disparate parts of our psyche. When we exile our anger, vulnerability, ambition, etc. we push them into the shadows where they operate unconsciously, often sabotaging our conscious intention.

When we lack self-security, we're constantly scanning our environment for threats to our self-worth. It traps us in a stagnation loop. Every criticism feels personal. Every failure feels like proof of our fundamental inadequacy. We either inflate ourselves defensively or deflate ourselves preemptively, but either way, we're not present to reality as it actually is.

Pursuing Transcendence

Self-security provides the foundation, but genuine flourishing requires us to move beyond ourselves. This is what psychologist Abraham Maslow called self-transcendence, the highest human need he identified. Interestingly, he added this to his hierarchy of needs only later in life, recognizing that peak human experience comes not from self-actualization alone, but from dedicating ourselves to something larger than ourselves.

Naval Ravikant puts it bluntly:

"Me is a disease."

Self-obsession is a primary source of unhappiness and misery. When we're trapped in constant rumination about our own status, problems, and validation, we create our own suffering. The paradox of transcendence is this: the less we focus on ourselves, the more we experience deeper satisfaction and the more we become more fully ourselves.

Psychologist Alfred Adler understood this deeply. He argued that the key to happiness lies in contributing to your community. This could mean raising children, volunteering at a homeless shelter, embarking on a mission to impact billions, or serving God. The specific form doesn't matter. What matters is that your energy flows outward toward something beyond your own ego.

Transcendence doesn't require self-sacrifice. It's somehow both self-indulgent and selfless. When you serve something larger than yourself, it feels good. And that's ok!

Integration

Turning this into practice is where it gets messy. But thinking does nothing. It's only when you engage with reality (when you test yourself against real challenges, real feedback, real relationships) that you actually grow. This is the most important part of the process towards flourishing.

For self-security, start by understanding where you are insecure now. Do you feel confident in your ability to handle criticism without taking it personally? What about sitting with discomfort without immediately seeking distraction or validation? These require honest self-assessment.

Notice when you're performing for approval versus acting from genuine conviction. Notice when you avoid challenges because failure might confirm your worst beliefs about yourself.

For transcendence, what path have you actually taken toward something beyond yourself? It's easy to say you value contribution, but have you tested that claim? What have you chosen as your outlet for transcendence? Are you trying to check yourself when your ego gets too big?

The only way to know if your sense of purpose is real is to engage with it, put yourself in situations where your contribution matters and see what happens.

Here are some practices to implement into your own life:

  • Seek honest feedback. Find people who will tell you the truth because they care enough to be honest. Ask them how you're showing up & listen without defending.
  • Get uncomfortable. Try things that push you beyond your circle of competence. Apply for things you might not get. Have difficult conversations. Risk looking foolish. This is where growth happens.
    • Self-security means challenging yourself, not just filling your needs
    • Pursuing transcendence is creating real impact rather than tweaking "internal states" (how you feel)
  • Catch yourself in the moment. When you notice yourself triggered or reactive, pause and ask: "What part of me is feeling threatened right now? What does it need?" This creates space between stimulus and response.
  • Do shadow work. Notice what qualities in others trigger strong reactions. That person's arrogance that irritates you? Probably a disowned part of your own healthy self-assertion. That person's vulnerability you're drawn to? Likely your own exiled tenderness calling to be integrated.
  • Regulate your nervous system. Learn the difference between your sympathetic (fight/flight/fawn/flock) and parasympathetic (rest/digest) states. Notice when you're stuck in chronic activation or numbed-out shutdown. Develop practices that help you return to regulation.
  • Practice somatic awareness. Go for walks without music, just you and an open notes app. Try yoga, breathwork, meditation. Your body knows things your mind hasn't caught up to yet. These practices help you develop a felt sense of security and presence that goes beyond intellectual understanding.
  • Create something. Whether through writing, art, music, or movement, creative expression allows different parts of you to speak. What emerges often surprises you, revealing aspects you didn't know were there.

All of these practices follow the same pattern: engage with reality, get feedback, adjust, repeat. This process of testing your ideas and growth against real-world feedback is crucial. Without it, self-security becomes self-delusion and transcendence becomes spiritual bypassing.

There's a LOT here so I've created a couple frameworks around how you can implement these principles in your life! These will come as essays in the future2!

Relationships as a Testing Ground

Genuine connection is where both pillars of flourishing converge and reveal themselves most clearly.

Without self-security, your relationships become transactional. You're constantly managing how others perceive you, reading into their words for validation or rejection, performing rather than being present.

Without transcendence, you risk becoming self-absorbed, treating relationships as extensions of your inner work rather than true encounters with other people.

Real connection requires both: showing up authentically (security) while genuinely caring about the other person's experience, not just your own (transcendence).

Pay attention to your patterns. They're feedback mechanisms showing you exactly where your foundation needs work.

Do you consistently attract people who treat you poorly? That's feedback about your self-worth. Do you withdraw when things get difficult? That's feedback about your capacity for intimacy and vulnerability. Do you need constant reassurance? That's feedback about your security. Do you keep score or need credit for everything you do? That's feedback about whether you've truly moved beyond ego.

Relationships are also opportunities to practice transcendence. When you stop making every interaction about yourself (Am I good enough? Do they like me? Am I winning?), you can actually show up for others. You can listen without planning your response. You can support without needing credit. You can unconditionally love.

Don't be afraid to test different ways of being in relationships. Try expressing more vulnerability. Try setting firmer boundaries. Try being more playful, or more serious, or more direct. Notice what feels authentic. Notice how people respond when you show up differently.

This is about discovering which parts of yourself you've been hiding and why.

The Ongoing Journey

When we develop genuine self-security and create a path towards transcendence, the effects ripple out into every area of our lives. Our relationships become more authentic3 because we're no longer performing for approval. Our work becomes more creative because we're not constrained by fear of failure. Our presence becomes more healing because we're not unconsciously projecting our shadows onto others.

Perhaps most importantly, we become capable of genuine service. When we're not constantly trying to fill our own emptiness, we have energy available to contribute to something larger than ourselves.

But this is an ongoing process of deepening and integration.

What makes this journey worthwhile is the relief of not having to pretend to be someone we're not. It's the freedom and the joy of discovering that we're already whole, already worthy, already enough, and that from this foundation, genuine growth and contribution become possible.

"The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are." — Carl Jung

As I continue on this path, I'm continually amazed by how much more there is to discover. Each layer of integration reveals new possibilities. Each deepening of self-security opens new capacities for connection and creation.

If you're reading this and feeling the call to explore these dimensions of your own experience, know that you're not alone on this journey. In that shared journey toward wholeness, we find not only our own flourishing but our capacity to support the flourishing of others.
